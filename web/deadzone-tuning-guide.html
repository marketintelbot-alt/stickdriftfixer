Tuning

Deadzone Tuning Without Killing Aim Precision

Use this method to reduce neutral drift while keeping low-speed stick control usable.

Why Deadzone Is a Tradeoff

Deadzone removes small input values around center. A larger deadzone hides drift more aggressively but also removes fine aiming movement. A smaller deadzone preserves precision but may allow visible drift. The right value is the minimum amount that stabilizes neutral input in your real play conditions.

Practical Tuning Sequence

  1. Calibrate with hands off the sticks.
  2. Start with low deadzone values and test neutral stability.
  3. Increase in small increments until unwanted movement stops.
  4. Retest precision aiming at low stick deflection.
  5. Save and label profile by controller and date.

Keep per-controller profiles. Two controllers of the same model can still need different deadzones due to wear variance.

Signs You Should Stop Tuning and Repair

  • Drift returns immediately after each calibration pass.
  • Required deadzone becomes so high that aiming feels delayed.
  • One axis shows unstable center values across short retests.
  • Stick feel includes mechanical roughness or spring weakness.
